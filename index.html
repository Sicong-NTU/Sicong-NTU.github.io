Overview

Recent advancements in large multimodal models (LMMs) have significantly enhanced performance across diverse tasks, with ongoing efforts to further integrate additional modalities such as video and audio. However, most existing LMMs remain vulnerable to hallucinations, the discrepancy between the factual multi-modal input and the generated textual output, which has limited their applicability in various real-world scenarios. This paper presents the first systematic investigation of hallucinations in LMMs involving the three most common modalities: language, visual, and audio. Our work distinguishes from existing benchmarks through four key features:

  • Essential Multi-Modalities: We analyze hallucinations and evaluate LMMs across the three fundamental modalities: Language, Visual, and Audio.
  • Systematic Hallucination Investigation: Our study reveals two key contributors to hallucinations: overreliance on unimodal priors and spurious inter-modality correlations.
  • Comprehensive Diagnosis: CMM defines hallucinations with nuanced subcategories and granularities, enabling comprehensive diagnosis of LMM vulnerabilities across various modalities.
  • Quality in Annotations: All data are newly annotated by humans, not from any existing video dataset, ensuring diversity and quality.

Examples

Examples of over-reliance hallucinations in CMM.

Question: "Did you see shoes are worn on feet in the video?"
Answer: "No."
This is a language domain over-reliance hallucination. The model hallucinates the wearing of shoes on feet because the shoes are usually worn on feet in common sense.

Question: "Did you hear hammer hitting in the audio?"
Answer: "No."
This is a visual domain over-reliance hallucination. The model hallucinates the hitting of hammer because the hammer appears in the video although there is only whisperring sound and no sound of hitting.

Question: "Did you see thunder in the video?"
Answer: "No."
This is a audio domain over-reliance hallucination. The model hallucinates the thunder because the thunder sound appears in the audio, but there is no thunder in the video.



Examples of inter-modality hallucinations in CMM.

Question: "Did you see trees in this video?"
Answer: "No."
This is a example of spurious Visual-Audio-Language (VAL) Correlations. The model hears birds chirping in the audio and may hallucinate seeing trees because of the high co-occurrence of "bird chirping" in audio captions and "trees" in corresponding video captions in the video-audio-text joint pretraining data.

Question: "Did you see a phone in this video?"
Answer: "No."
This is a example of spurious Visual-Language (VL) Correlations. While a person is visible in the video, the model may hallucinate the presence of a phone due to the frequent co-occurrence of "person" and "phone" in the video-text pre-training data.

Question: "Did you hear a dog barking in the audio?"
Answer: "No."
This is a example of spurious Audio-Language (AL) Correlations. While the audio just contains a dog whimpering, the model may hallucinate the sound of barking due to the frequent co-occurrence of "dog" and "barking" during audio-text pretraining.
